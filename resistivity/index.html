Maple Ridge sits at roughly 50 meters above sea level on the north bank of the Fraser River, where glacial till and alluvial deposits create a layered subsurface that changes drastically within a few hundred meters. With over 90,000 residents and a building stock expanding into the Albion and Silver Valley areas, knowing what lies beneath the surface before excavation or foundation design is not optional. Electrical resistivity surveys, particularly Vertical Electrical Sounding (VES), map these transitions by measuring how soil and rock resist current flow: saturated silts conduct, while dry gravel bars and bedrock resist. The method is non-invasive, fast to deploy, and in a municipality bounded by the river to the south and the Coast Mountains to the north, it answers the question every contractor and geotechnical engineer asks: where does the competent bearing stratum actually begin? In our experience, combining VES with a CPT test program on river-adjacent sites speeds up the ground model, because the resistivity profile flags soft clay lenses the cone might punch through without warning.

A resistivity contrast of 15 ohm-m to 800 ohm-m across a gravel-silt contact leaves no room for interpretation error.

Scope of work

What we see repeatedly in Maple Ridge is a thin crust of desiccated clay or compacted fill overlying water-charged silts that extend down to a buried gravel channel or directly to glacial till. Standard VES arrays, typically Schlumberger with AB/2 spacings reaching 100 to 150 meters, resolve these boundaries clearly. The resistivity contrast between a wet silt at 15 ohm-m and a dry gravel at 800 ohm-m is impossible to miss. We run the survey along proposed building footprints, utility corridors, and slope benches where the Golden Ears foothills transition to the floodplain. Data interpretation follows ASTM D6431-18 guidelines, with inversion modeling that corrects for the lateral variability so common near the Kanaka Creek and Fraser River confluences. For sites where the bedrock profile is steep, pairing VES with seismic refraction gives us both the electrical and mechanical boundaries, a combination that resolves ambiguities when the water table mimics a rock signature.

Area-specific notes

A six-storey mixed-use project on Dewdney Trunk Road hit a buried peat pocket at 8 meters depth that the initial borehole logs missed because the spacing was too wide. The excavation flooded, the shoring deflected, and the general contractor lost three weeks while a dewatering and redesign plan was rushed through. That peat, fully saturated and electrically conductive, would have shown as a sub-10 ohm-m anomaly on a VES profile run before the first shovel went in. Maple Ridge has these organic lenses scattered across the lowlands, remnants of old sloughs and side channels of the Fraser River. A resistivity survey costs a fraction of a day of downtime on a mid-size excavation, and it covers the gaps between boreholes. When the target is a gravel aggregate resource, missing a clay seam means crushing contaminated material; when it is a foundation, missing an aquitard means a sump pump running for the life of the building. The risk is not theoretical: we have remediated enough flooded pits in this municipality to know the pattern.

Q&A

How much does a VES survey cost for a typical Maple Ridge building lot?

For a standard residential or small commercial lot in Maple Ridge, a VES survey with 4 to 6 soundings typically ranges from CA$950 to CA$1,550, depending on access, line length, and whether we are correlating with existing borehole data. Larger sites with deeper investigation targets or difficult terrain near the Fraser River escarpment may fall outside that range.

How does VES differ from seismic refraction for bedrock mapping?

VES measures electrical resistivity, which responds strongly to water content and clay mineralogy; seismic refraction measures compressional wave velocity, which responds to mechanical stiffness. In Maple Ridge's saturated silts, VES often sees the water table as a sharp drop in resistivity, while seismic refraction sees it as a velocity increase. We frequently run both methods together because bedrock that is fractured and wet can look electrically like clay, but mechanically like rock: the two datasets resolve the ambiguity.

What depth can a VES survey reach in the Fraser Valley sediments?

With a Schlumberger array using AB/2 current electrode spacings of 150 meters, we can typically image to depths of 50 to 70 meters in the alluvial and glacial deposits common to Maple Ridge. Greater depth is possible with longer spreads, but practical limits are set by property boundaries, road crossings, and the need for a stable current injection. For targets deeper than 80 meters, we often complement VES with electromagnetic methods.

How long does a VES field survey take and what access is needed?

A crew of two can complete 6 to 8 soundings in a single day on open ground. Each sounding takes 20 to 40 minutes depending on the maximum electrode spacing. We need pedestrian access along the survey line; the equipment is lightweight and does not damage pavement or landscaping. In built-up areas like downtown Maple Ridge, we schedule work during low-traffic hours and coordinate with municipal permits where necessary.
